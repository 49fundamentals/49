CURRENCY

The loonie continued to weaken significantly against the US dollar, with CADUSD (sells CAD, buys USD) dropping 7.53% during January to 0.79. Investors are fleeing the currency - down 11.3% y/y - in search of better returns elsewhere. Lower prices for crude oil, a strong US dollar, falling rates and energy-exposed capital markets give little incentive for free-moving capital to shiver in Canadian banks. Returns are hotter south of 49°.

GDP

Growth in Canada continues to outpace the majority of G7 countries, though forecasts over the past few years have been consistenly over-optimistic.

"From the outset of the Great Recession, the BoC has been providing significant monetary stimulus. But we have yet to reach the point where growth is self-sustaining. For that to happen, the sources of growth will have to rotate away from consumption and toward increased exports, which are our traditional economic engine."
- Timothy Lane, Deputy Governor, Bank of Canada

INTEREST RATES

The Bank of Canada surprised markets at its January Monetary Policy Review by unexpectedly lowering the benchmark Overnight Rate a quarter of a percent to 0.75%. Governor Poloz described this rate cut as "insurance" to mitigate the impact that collapsing oil prices will have on Canadian businesses - especially those with exposure to the energy industry. The market has priced in a ~25% chance of a further rate cut in March, and a ~75% chance within the next 6 months.

Canadian banks followed suit - though with uncharacteristic delay. The Big Five lowered their prime rates from 3.00% to 2.85%, which is the first time in history that prime shifted by less than 0.25. Some analysts anticipate further cuts to prime in the spring, when housing market demand heats up.

The move reflects "our actual cost of funds and helps clients save money on products such as variable-rate mortgages, lines of credit and floating-rate loans."
- RBC

SECTOR INDICATORS

EMPLOYMENT

Canada created 121,000 jobs in 2014. In a country of ~35 million people (61.3% of whom work), that's not a lot of job growth (0.7%, to be exact).

But, don't fret - Canada's unemployment rate finished 2014 at 6.6%! Even better: using U.S. methodology, our unemployment rate is 5.7% (U.S.: 5.6%).

CONSUMERS

Heavily indebted consumers are benefiting from lower oil prices and downward-trending interest rates. Cheaper gasoline reaches consumers faster than businesses, since corporate buyers generally obtain energy through forward fixed-price contracts.

12 per cent of Canadian households ahve a total debt-to-income ratio above 250 per cent, and although this percentage has been steady over the past few years, it is almost double the level in 2000. These highly indebted households carry about 40% of overall household debt.

Automobile-related debt has nearly doubled over the past 8 years, fueled further by low interest rates. As of Q2 2014, about 25% of total auto loans oustanding were non-prime.

One-third of Canadian households have no debt at all. This proportion has been relatively constant over the past decasde.

HOUSING

The Bank of Canada recently published research suggesting that Canadian homes are overvalued by up to 30% - relative to incomes, interest rates, and housing market cycles around the globe. December's Financial System Review declared that the Bank sees the probability of a housing market correction to be low, though Governor Stephen Poloz noted that "we don't see ourselves as targeting housing markets in any way."

COMMERCIAL PROPERTY

EXPORTS

As the United States reduces its reliance on foreign sources of oil, Canadian crude continues to flow across the border in larger quantities. Nearly 100% of Canadian oil exports go to the U.S., which gives Canadian producers a 40% market share of U.S. crude oil imports.

INFRASTRUCTURE

Canadian energy exports continue to be constrained by under-investments in trade infrastructure. Deep sea ports on the Pacific coast remain underbuilt; cross-country pipelines such as Energy East continue to face opposition; and capacity-boosting Keystone XL remains mired in politics.

Canada's business friendly government continues to pursue global trade partnerships: concluding negotiations on the ambitious 'Canada-European Union: Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement', which is expected to come into effect in 2016.

REGULATION

In the December 2014 Financial System Review, the BoC identified high household indebtedness, imbalances in the housing market, and increased investor risk-taking as the 3 largest vulnerabilities facing Canada's financial system.

In recent months the Federal government has inched closer to the establishment of a national securities regulator, with Ontario and British Columbia signalling their desire to part with the existing 13-regulator system. Canada remains the only major industrialized country without a national capital markets regulator.

FINANCES

Finance Minister Joe Oliver has pushed the 2015-2016 budget's release date to April 2015, saying that more time was needed to judge the effects of significantly lower oil prices.

Plummeting prices for crude oil has placed the Conservative governments commitment to balancing the Federal budget in 2015-2016 under pressure. The government's Economic and Fiscal Projections (Nov 2014) was modelled with oil prices at $81, and was expected to allow for a $3B reserve fund. The Conservatives hoped to pay down federal debt with these monies, but it looks like these reserves will instead be used to cover revenue shortfalls resulting from lower oil prices.
